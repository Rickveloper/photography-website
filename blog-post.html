Chasing Winter Storms

January 15, 2023 • 8 min read

Frozen shoreline at Pemaquid Point during a nor'easter

The forecast called for -15°F with 50mph winds when I set out for Pemaquid Point at 4am. My fingers were already numb loading the car, but I knew the reward would be worth the discomfort. Winter storms transform the Maine coast into something primal and untamed—exactly what I wanted to capture.

The Gear That Survived

Shooting in these conditions requires more than just warm clothing. Here's what made the cut:

  • Canon EOS R5 with battery grip (extra batteries kept in interior pockets)
  • RF 24-70mm f/2.8 and RF 70-200mm f/2.8 lenses
  • Heavy-duty tripod with sandbag weight
  • Lens heater bands to prevent condensation freezing
  • Neoprene camera cover for snow protection
"The cold reveals what summer hides—the bones of the land laid bare under ice and snow."

Finding Composition in Chaos

At first glance, a winter storm scene appears as pure visual noise. Waves crash unpredictably, snow blows sideways, and ice forms erratic patterns. The key is to isolate elements:

I positioned myself behind a natural windbreak of granite boulders, using the 70-200mm to compress perspective on the lighthouse. Shooting at 1/1000s froze the wave action while the falling snow created natural motion blur. The resulting image juxtaposes the steadfast man-made structure against nature's fury.

The Shot That Almost Didn't Happen

After three hours, my LCD screen started flickering—a sure sign the cold was winning. I had one last composition in mind: an intimate study of ice formations on the tidal rocks. Switching to the 24-70mm at f/11 for depth of field, I framed the abstract patterns created by successive freeze-thaw cycles.

The camera shut down mid-sequence. Wrapping it against my body for ten minutes provided just enough warmth to capture this final frame before retreating to the car. Sometimes the best images demand everything you have to give.

Lessons From The Edge

Winter coastal photography teaches resilience. What I've learned:

  • Shoot RAW—you'll need the latitude to correct white balance later
  • Overexpose by 1/3 stop to compensate for snow's reflectivity
  • Use manual focus (autofocus struggles with snow)
  • Wool glove liners allow dexterity while protecting skin
  • Always have an emergency exit strategy

The images from that morning now hang in a Portland gallery, but what stays with me is the memory of standing alone at the continent's edge, bearing witness to nature's raw power. That's the real reward.
